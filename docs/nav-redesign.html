otium — navigation redesign

An audit of today's information architecture and two alternative navigation models to explore, as wireframes. Low-fi on purpose — this is about structure, not styling. July 2026.

The root problem: 11 destinations are squeezed into 2 bottom tabs, a crammed Library header, and 8 different modal/sheet components. Everything the two tabs can't hold spilled into content headers (overflow) and into modals (scroll-fight, wasted space). The fix isn't more polish — it's a flatter, page-based IA where each screen does one thing and every destination has an obvious home.

1 · What we have today

Bottom nav: Intent · Library — only two. Everything else (Collections, History, Feed mix, Settings, Appearance, Import, Manage, Add, Refresh, feed/state filters, sort) is reached from inside the Library header or a Manage sheet. Detail lives on real pages (source, feed) but is often entered through a modal. Content viewing (reader/player) and most actions are sheets over a scrollable page.

2 · Redesign principles

Pages, not modals

Navigate to a full page (push → back) for anything with content or controls. One scroll context, room to breathe, a clear back. Reserve sheets for one genuinely-transient thing (a confirm, a single quick action).

A nav that actually holds the app

Give the primary destinations a real top-level home so nothing spills into content headers. The Library becomes just the library.

One job per page

Each screen is narrow and focused. Management, browsing, reading, and settings are separate places, not one crowded surface.

Settings & secondary = one obvious tap

Settings, appearance, insights, import, history live in a clear place — never buried behind a content menu.

3 · Two navigation models

Both apply the principles above (pages over modals; clean Library). They differ in how the destinations are held. Flip between them — swipe the phone strip sideways to see each screen.

Read · Library · Saved · You — a conventional 4-tab bar. Every destination has a tab; details push as pages. Familiar, scales, kills the header overflow, settings is one tap.

4 · Recommendation

Lead with Model A (4 tabs) + the pages-not-modals pass. It most directly kills your two biggest complaints — the Library-header overflow (every destination gets its own tab) and the scroll-fight (content lives on pages, not sheets) — and it makes Settings a one-tap, obvious place. Model B is the fallback if you want to protect the ultra-minimal 2-tab chrome; it solves the same problems but trades a second tap and leans on one hub button.

Concretely, whichever model: Library becomes just a sources/feeds list + one filter entry; Collections + History move together (Saved / hub); Settings · Appearance · Insights(mix) · Import get a home (You / hub); the reader/player + all management convert from sheets to pushed pages; sheets survive only for confirms + the save picker + quick weight. Net: fewer components, no overflow, no scroll-fight, each page narrow and calm.
