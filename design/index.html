Finding otium's face

Eighteen ways the same app could look and feel. Same content, same interactions - only the design language changes. Read top to bottom, drag the pads, and pick the one (or the mix) that feels like ours.

Right now the app works but looks like every app - a bland default that could be anyone's. The point of this pass is to find a face that's unmistakably otium: calm, deliberate, a little classical, built for a thumb on a phone. Not "a feed with nicer colors," a genuinely distinct experience.

So I had a fleet of agents each build a complete mobile prototype in one committed design language - no half-measures, no hedging toward the middle. Every prototype shows the same three screens (the intent pad, a session, the library) with the same content, so you're comparing design, not features. Each preview below is the real thing, running; tap it to open full-screen and actually drag the analog stick.

How to read this. Don't look for "the finished app" - these are committed sketches, some need contrast or polish. Judge the language: does this feel like a considered, unhurried reading ritual, or like another content firehose? You can also mix - "that palette, this typography, that pad." Just tell me where you land.

One thing they all share on purpose: the bones. The information architecture, the session-not-feed model, the analog-stick intent pad, the touch rules, and the anti-patterns (no infinite scroll, no unread badges, no streaks) are written up in the experience guidelines and hold no matter which skin wins. What follows is only the look and feel, grouped into four families.

So - how to decide

A few ways to cut it. If you want otium to feel timeless and trustworthy, the light-and-literary family (Reading Room, E-Ink) is the safe, durable bet. If you want it to feel special and yours the moment you open it, the crafted and luminous families (Analog Desk, Nocturne, Deco, Observatory) have the most personality without going loud. If "not another bland app" is the whole point and you want edge, the graphic family (Riso, Bauhaus, Swiss) is the antidote. And two of them - Blueprint and the Analog Desk - make the two-axis pad feel inevitable, which is worth weighting since the pad is our signature.

Tell me a single direction to make real, or a Frankenstein - "Nocturne's black-and-ember palette, Reading Room's typography, the Analog Desk pad." I'll wire the winning tokens into the live app so the instance on your phone becomes that design, then we iterate on the real thing.
